Respiratory System Effects

The respiratory system is the first biological system affected by poor air quality. Pollutants, dust particles and harmful gases can irritate the airway lining and reduce breathing efficiency.

  • Dry throat and coughing
  • Chest tightness and wheezing
  • Increased mucus production
  • Reduced oxygen exchange efficiency
  • Breathing discomfort during activity
  • Aggravation of asthma and COPD

Long-term exposure may contribute to chronic bronchitis, reduced lung capacity, persistent airway inflammation and increased susceptibility to respiratory infections.

Cardiovascular Effects

Air pollutants can trigger systemic inflammation and oxidative stress, increasing strain on the cardiovascular system.

  • Elevated heart rate
  • Increased blood pressure
  • Reduced exercise tolerance
  • Cardiovascular stress
  • Fatigue during physical activity

Long-term exposure may increase the risk of hypertension, coronary artery disease and stroke.

Neurological & Cognitive Effects

Poor air quality can affect concentration, mental performance and overall cognitive efficiency.

  • Brain fog
  • Headaches
  • Mental fatigue
  • Reduced concentration
  • Slower reaction times
  • Mood disturbances

Elevated CO₂ concentrations and reduced oxygen availability can impair cognitive function and decrease productivity.

Oxygen Deficiency Effects

  • Fatigue and drowsiness
  • Dizziness and headaches
  • Reduced physical performance
  • Rapid breathing
  • Confusion and poor coordination
  • Hypoxia in severe cases

Reduced oxygen concentration can significantly impact cellular function, physical performance and overall wellbeing.

Dust & Particulate Matter Impact

  • Eye irritation
  • Throat irritation
  • Sneezing and coughing
  • Respiratory inflammation
  • Reduced lung function
  • Increased asthma severity

Fine airborne particles can penetrate deep into lung tissue and contribute to long-term respiratory complications.

VOC Exposure Analysis

  • Eye and nose irritation
  • Headaches
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Respiratory discomfort
  • Chemical sensitivity

Long-term exposure to elevated VOC concentrations may increase chronic respiratory irritation and negatively affect indoor environmental quality.

Temperature & Humidity Effects

High Humidity

Can encourage mold growth, dust mites and allergen accumulation, increasing respiratory discomfort.

Low Humidity

May cause dry skin, dry throat, nasal irritation and respiratory tract discomfort.

Extreme Temperature

Can increase physiological stress, reduce comfort and place additional strain on respiratory and cardiovascular systems.

High-Risk Populations

Children

Higher breathing rates and developing lungs make children more susceptible to pollutant exposure.

Elderly Individuals

Reduced respiratory reserve and increased cardiovascular sensitivity increase vulnerability.

Asthma Patients

Poor air quality may trigger asthma attacks and increase airway inflammation.

COPD Patients

Reduced lung function can make even moderate air quality conditions difficult to tolerate.

Pregnant Individuals

Environmental stressors may increase physiological burden and sensitivity to hypoxic conditions.

Recommended Actions

  • Good: Normal activity.
  • Satisfactory: Maintain ventilation.
  • Moderate: Increase airflow and monitor sensitive individuals.
  • Poor: Reduce prolonged exposure.
  • Very Poor: Use air filtration and limit activity.
  • Severe: Immediate intervention and exposure reduction recommended.
